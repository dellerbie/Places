      6/14/2011

      My boy from Nigeria was craving some home cooking one day and so we took a trip to this little spot. He told me it's good and big portions. So I'm like, alite! I'm in! First of all, this was my first time to this place and I've always heard of him talking about jollof rice. So I ordered a combo plate with jollof rice, dodo (fried plantains), and a some kind of mixed stew with mixed meats. My boy got the Egusi soup with fufu. All I have to say is that everything had a rich flavorful taste. The rice was a little spicy, but delicious. Although the mixed meat was a bit chewy, I will admit the sauce from the stew was great too and the meat tastes fresh.

      I had some of the Egusi soup with fufu and it was good too. The Egusi soup tasted like a curry flavor type of soup. The fufu was a huge white dough like a huge piece of bread in which you take chunks off the fufu, roll it up, and dip it into the Egusi. When I saw this, I was like: "you get to play with your food like a little kid and eat it at the same time?" Cool!

      Basically let me just sum it up for you: big portions to the point where I had leftovers for 2 additional meals. The food is a little spicy, but when you wash it down with a Vita Malt drink, which is tastes like a sweet barely drink, it all tastes good. Although you might have to wait a little longer than some other places for the food; everything that you taste from here is rich and full of flavors. Plus, Veronica will most likely come and talk to you before you leave.

      Review from Tuan D.

      • Elite '11

      • Tuan D.

      Torrance, CA

      9/2/2010

      Walking into this place gave me the nostalgic feeling of walking into my friend's kitchen while his mom was cooking up some Korean food for me to try for the first time.  I was confused but excited.  Suffice to say I was quite nervous due to this being a new cuisine and the many (if any) etiquettes associated with it....and of course the huge portrait of a Nigerian woman in traditional clothing on the wall.  Our waitress was very lovely and patient with us.  Knowing that it was our first time trying Nigerian food, she recommended the "safe" dishes like jollof rice with soup, some greens and grains.  But no no no....we did our research and ordered fufu with egusi with goat meat, jollof rice with fish and goat meat, another jollof rice with chicken stew, AND a goat pepper soup in addition to some meat pies for appetizers.  We were a hungry bunch and the waitress got a good laugh at it.  

      Yes the pace of this place is kind of slow but I wouldn't use slow to describe it (unless you have a noon executive meeting in which your entire professional career depends on it).  It was very relax.  We just talked and talked and before we knew it, the dishes were presented on the table.  The portion was big enough to feed a small company.  Here's a rundown:

      -Jollof rice - huge portion.  I wouldn't say it was like any of the rice I've tasted before.  The spiced flavor reminded me of Mayan/Yucatean cuisine that I got to try in Cancun.  It was very distinct and definitely a must-try.

      -Dodo aka fried plantains that came with the rice - I don't think they were fried at all because there was no greasiness which was a plus.  I used it gratuitously as a good palate cleanser in between dishes.

      -Stews - These tomato-based stews were excellent accompaniment to the jollof rice and fufu.  I could tell that they employed a tremendous amount of natural flavors to bring about these soups.  The chicken pieces were fried before they were added to the soup.  You could taste the crispiness but not the crispy texture.  Delicious!

      -Egusi soup with fufu - The main attraction, the reason I decided to visit this place after watching a youtube clip of Nigerian women making fufu the traditional way.  I was expecting the fufu to be glutinous similar to a soft mochi.  The actual texture was somewhat between mochi and mashed potatoes while the taste was almost similar to mashed potatoes.  It was nonetheless in perfect combination with the egusi soup, which to me was a richer, nuttier, flavor-induced pumpkin soup.

      -Goat Pepper Soup - Beware that this is not just goat meat but the offal as well.  We could see some liver and may be stomach pieces.  The guy who ordered it loved it but it was too much for me.  Though the soup itself was good.

      The six of us couldn't finish everything (including one guy I swear could have eaten a small goat in one sitting).  Leftovers included a full stew, a full plate of jollof, and one fufu batch.  Right when we were accessing the financial damage that we'd done, the bill came out much less than possible.  With tax and tips, each of us only had to contribute $11.  It was quite a satisfying meal which left my stomach and wallet happy.

      I grew up on Nigerian food and each time I come here, I get taken back to those youthful days around the dinner table with my siblings and parents. As little girl my siblings and I would joke and say, "Eat your fufu, oh! If you don't behave, I will flog you this night, oh!" Then another sibling would jokingly plead, "Hafia! Eh-eh!" (Leave him or her alone!)

      Anyway, Veronica's Kitchen serves a great variety of meals and offers drinks straight from Nigeria (palm wine).  I travel all the way from Orange County to eat at this great restaurant.  It's far worth the drive.  I've had just about every soup on the menu and all are delicious. I've also had the Jollof Rice, Plantains, and the Moi Moi (bean cake - it's like a tamale).  One word describes these dishes - scrumptious...For first timers who have never eaten a Nigerian meal, perhaps try the Jollof Rice with Plantains and the Malta drink.  The rice is similar to Spanish rice in appearance, but is spiced differently with curry and red pepper being the spices that stand out. It is not too spicy so if you like it hot, ask for extra red pepper.

      If one would like to delve deeper, I recommend trying any of the soups with fufu, which is pounded cassava or similar starch. I get my soups with steamed tilapia fish, as I am a pescatarian and I don't eat meat. Made this style, one must beware of bone. If you swallow bone, take three fingers full of fufu and swallow it!

      The place itself is unassuming and can be further described as a "whole in the wall" joint with absolutely fantastic food. It feels like Nigeria with the music playing or at times a Nigerian movie rolling on the flat screen. You must go in knowing that they will be operating on African Time. In other words, if you're in a rush or on your lunch break, phone-in your order and leave another 25-40 minutes before you go in to dine on the order.  

      The portions are generous, so expect to pack plenty of food for subsequent meals.

      This review is for the Manchester Ave location, not Lake Balboa.

      Finally, a legitimate reason to blame my Dad for something!!! Its all his fault that I got on this African cuisine kick and boy am I glad I did.  This hole-in-the-wall joint packs a serious punch with friendliness and lotsa down right good cookin'.  

      Once inside, I felt like I was in an out of the ways local joint while on vacation. Its clean and comfy, kinda feels like someone's front living room converted into a dining area, lol.

      We were greeted by Marie (see pic) who was incredibly patient with the big crowd I came in with.  The menu isn't descriptive at all unless you read/speak Nigerian causing all of us to pepper Marie with a gazillion questions.  She was so sweet and patient with us, all the while flashing that great big warm smile of hers.  

      Here's what we had: (Check out my pics.)

      * Jollof Rice (looks like spanish rice) that comes with the goat meat stew that I don't know the name of.That was yummy!

      * Plaintains are called DoDo, and the traditional chicken dish which I also don't know the name of.

      * The greens were one of my favorites!!! Its called Efo which is made out of collard greens or spinach with tomato paste and onions. YUM!! Has a nice kick to this.

      * The white mounds remind me of Chinese baos, but they are actually pounded yams called FuFu. These were strange when eaten on their own, but a very when combined with the greens or goat stew.

      * This is called Egusi, which is made out of seeds from the egusi vegetable. This one had a kick! a very strong taste and "personality" as I call it. If I just had white rice to eat with this, it would have been PERFECT! And a set of chopsticks. Wait, I'm having Nigerian food. Truly, this dish was AMAZING!!  

      * Palm wine. It smelled and looked very "fermented" meaning it didn't seem very processed. Kinda reminds me of what moonshine alcohol must have been like.  I'll pass on this one the next time, but very glad to have had tried it.

      NOTE :  We did have to ask for silverware and extra plates constantly.  It could be a cultural difference.  Service was a bit slower than we're used to, but you could tell they were doing the best they could.  I think we may have overwhelmed them with our 20+ group size.  They really were soo nice, and seemed very grateful for the business.  I think they were touched by our genuine eagerness to familiarize ourselves with their food as well.

      Hole in the wall place for sure. Don't go if you're looking for ambiance, etc. Service was very warm and hospitable. :) Food was DELISH!     Our bill for all the above including individual drinks came out to about $18 per person with tax and tip.

      My cousin and another friend of mine checked it out a few weeks after I initially tried this place back in August, and I believe both their parties loved it too!

      Came here with 5 friends (one of whom is of Nigerian descent) on a Friday night. We ordered chinchin-a sweet, crunchy cinnamon treat, meat pies, egusi (a stew with different meats and spices), fufu (african dough ball- similar consistency  to japanese mochi), jollof (similar to spanish rice), plaintains, chicken with red sauce,  and goat stew.  I'm not a huge fan of goat (cooked in ANY way), but everything else was absolutely DELICIOUS! The portions were huge and each of us took something home with us. Pocket hit- about $15 per person. That covers one cocktail in a snooty Hollywood restaurant!! The only reason i can't give the place 5 stars is that this was my first time trying Nigerian food so i can't compare it to anything else.
      Yes, you're in Inglewood.  Yes, the restaurant is tiny. Yes, the staff is SUPER casual (think flip flops)..and YES, the food is totally yummy. Expand your palate, people. Go fufu!
